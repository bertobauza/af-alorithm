1. AF-CARE Framework Overview

▼

The 2024 ESC/EACTS Guidelines introduce the AF-CARE framework — a structured, patient-centred approach organising care into four integrated pillars. All patients with AF should be managed according to ALL four pillars simultaneously.

Landiolol (ultra-short acting beta-blocker) is referenced in ESC guidelines for acute rate control in critically ill patients with low ejection fraction. Landiolol is NOT widely available in Australian hospitals. Use esmolol as the Australian equivalent short-acting beta-blocker where available, or IV metoprolol/amiodarone.

10. Bleeding Risk Management (HAS-BLED Deprecated)

▼
HAS-BLED and all bleeding risk scores should NOT be used to decide on starting or withdrawing OAC in patients with AF III-B. Bleeding risk factors are rarely a reason to withdraw or withhold OAC, as the risk of stroke without anticoagulation often outweighs the risk of major bleeding.

Vernakalant (Brinavess) is a Class I recommendation in the ESC guidelines for pharmacological cardioversion but is NOT approved by the TGA and is NOT available in Australian hospitals. In Australia, flecainide and amiodarone remain the primary pharmacological cardioversion agents.
Ibutilide is referenced in ESC guidelines for cardioversion (especially atrial flutter). Ibutilide has limited availability in Australia — check local formulary. If unavailable, use flecainide or amiodarone.

17. Trigger-Induced AF (Sepsis / Infection / Acute Illness)

▼

Definition: New AF in close proximity to a precipitating and potentially reversible factor. The most common precipitant is acute sepsis (AF prevalence 9–20%, worse prognosis).
